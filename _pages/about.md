#### Science
I'm in the second year of my PhD at Stanford University, advised by [James Zou](https://www.james-zou.com). I'm currently working on improving computational tools to predict which cancer mutations will be presented to the immune system and available for targeting with immunotherapies. I'm also working on models to predict the set of antigens a T cell can bind to, to help identify what drives T cells to attack non-foreign cells in autoimmune diseases and T cell therapy off-target attacks. Previously I've work on cancer bioinformatics, protein language models, and computational small molecule drug design.


#### Advocacy
The other side of my career is focused on cancer patient advocacy. When I was 12, I had a rare pediatric liver cancer ([Fibrolamellar Hepatocellular Carcinoma](https://www.cancer.gov/pediatric-adult-rare-tumor/rare-tumors/rare-digestive-system-tumors/fibrolamellar-hepatocellular-carcinoma)), which motivated me to get into cancer reserach in the first place. I initiated a project to understand basic questions that I, as a patient, had about FLC. After collecting, sequencing, and analyzing FLC tumors, we identified the driver mutation of the cancer. When this was published, there was a surprisingly large press response, which created a platform for me to become a patient advocate and bring attention to causes that were, and still are, underprioritized. My goals are to increase funding for rare and pediatric disease research, clearly communicate academic research to patient communities, ensure research goals align with patient needs, and create opportunities for patients to get involved in research. I'm one of the directors of the [Fibrolamellar Registry](https://fibroregistry.org/about-the-fibrolamellar-registry/), a non-profit run by FLC patients and families. We work with FLC community members to curate a registry of patients' tumor samples and health data that is available for researchers, to address the sparsity of FLC data available at a given institution, and accelerate reserach. We also create resources that explain the results of FLC research back to the community in layman's terms.
